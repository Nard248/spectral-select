Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, "Unsupervised Deep Learning for Wavelength Selection in Multi-Excitation Fluorescence Imaging," for consideration in Communications AI & Computing. Fluorescence imaging reveals molecular signatures that are invisible to the eye, and it has become a valuable tool across the natural and medical sciences: environmental scientists use lichen autofluorescence to monitor air quality and ecosystem health, while in medicine, because tissues differ in their fluorescent signatures, the same principle can guide a surgeon toward a tumor margin, reveal radiofrequency ablation lesions in real time, or characterize tissue without dyes or a biopsy. Multi-excitation hyperspectral imaging extends this by varying the illuminating wavelength, turning each pixel into a full excitation–emission fingerprint that separates materials ordinary imaging cannot. This richness comes at a cost: it multiplies the data per pixel into hundreds of layers, straining acquisition, storage, and computation, and existing band-selection tools treat wavelengths in isolation and miss the cross-excitation structure that makes the modality powerful.

We present an entirely unsupervised framework that addresses this gap: a convolutional autoencoder with parallel excitation branches learns a compact representation of the data, a perturbation analysis attributes importance to individual excitation–emission pairs, and a relevance–diversity criterion selects a minimal, non-redundant subset. On lichens and on collagen sponges—a tissue-engineering material whose crosslinking mirrors the structural changes clinicians care about—the method matched or exceeded full-spectrum accuracy while retaining as few as 9 of 192 wavelengths, a reduction that translates directly into simpler sensors and faster, point-of-care analysis. We believe this work fits Communications AI & Computing because the journal welcomes machine-learning methodologies and their application to scientific discovery, as well as research that bridges artificial intelligence with related disciplines. Our contribution combines unsupervised representation learning with interpretable model attribution to produce a concrete, physically meaningful result—the wavelengths an instrument should acquire—and, although demonstrated here on fluorescence imaging, the approach is modality-agnostic and applicable to other high-dimensional, correlated sensor data. The work is original, is not published or under consideration elsewhere, and all authors have approved this submission and declare no competing interests. Thank you for your consideration; we look forward to your feedback.
